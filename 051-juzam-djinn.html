Entry 051 · Early Expansion Icons and Old-School Tournament Magic

Four Mana, Five Power, One Life

Juzám Djinn was the first great proof that a creature could be brutally undercosted when its controller accepted pain in return—an Old School finisher, a lesson in resource exchange, and eventually one of Magic’s most recognizable prestige cards.

I · The Bargain

Four Mana, Five Power, One Life

Juzám Djinn asks a question that became central to black’s identity: how much pain should a player accept in exchange for power delivered ahead of schedule? In 1993, the answer was startling. Four mana bought a 5/5 creature, and the payment plan was only one point of damage at the beginning of each upkeep.

The body was not merely large. It was large at the exact point on the curve where an aggressive deck wanted to stop preparing and start ending the game. Juzám survived a single Lightning Bolt, attacked through nearly every ordinary creature, and threatened twenty damage in four clean hits. The upkeep trigger looked dangerous on the card. In practice, it often measured how little time the opponent had left.

That exchange made Juzám Djinn more than an efficient threat. It taught players that life was not a score to preserve but a resource to convert. Long before Necropotence made the lesson explicit, Juzám put the principle into combat.

The original object

II · The Expansion

The first expansion needed a monster

Arabian Nights arrived only months after Magic itself. It was the game’s first expansion and its first demonstration that a new set could alter not merely the available card pool but the scale of what players imagined cards could do.

The set introduced lands with extraordinary utility, creatures drawn from the stories of Rabiah, and risks that did not resemble Alpha’s cautious exchange rates. Library of Alexandria converted a land drop into repeatable card advantage. City of Brass fixed every color at the cost of life. Serendib Efreet and Erhnam Djinn offered large bodies with unusual drawbacks. Juzám was the black culmination of that philosophy: an obviously dangerous permanent whose danger pointed in both directions.

It also looked like an expansion card. The Arabian Nights frame, scimitar symbol, compressed dark mana symbols, and Mark Tedin illustration made it immediately distinct from the core set. Juzám did not feel like another large creature. It felt like evidence that an unknown world beyond Alpha had already produced something stronger.

The upkeep damage was printed as a warning. Competitive players learned to read it as a discount.Black’s first great exchange rate
III · The Rate

What four mana bought in 1993

Modern evaluation can obscure how extreme Juzám Djinn appeared. The early creature curve was deliberately conservative. Large bodies generally cost five or six mana, carried severe restrictions, or exposed themselves to common removal.

Juggernaut

Four mana · 5/3

Equal power, two fewer toughness, forced attacks, and an inability to be blocked by Walls rather than genuine evasion.

Sengir Vampire

Five mana · 4/4 flying

Excellent in the era, but slower, smaller, and one full turn behind the Djinn’s pressure.

Serra Angel

Five mana · 4/4 flying, vigilance

A premier threat whose utility justified the extra mana, yet which lost a direct ground fight with Juzám.

Mahamoti Djinn

Six mana · 5/6 flying

A classic control finisher with superior evasion and toughness, purchased two turns later.

Juzám’s efficiency was not a minor improvement over the field. It compressed the size associated with a six-mana finisher into a four-mana creature. The only printed concession was a life payment that did not affect the battlefield.

Mark Tedin’s original Juzám DjinnThe creature leans into the frame with a grin that reads as appetite rather than triumph. Horns, smoke, shadow, and the close perspective make the card feel less like a distant monster and more like something that has already found the player.
IV · The Resource

Black taught players to spend life

New players naturally treat twenty life as something to defend. Juzám Djinn punishes that instinct. A player who refuses the card because it deals damage to its controller ignores the much larger quantity of damage it threatens to deal across the table.

If the Djinn attacks four times, it deals twenty damage while its controller ordinarily absorbs four upkeep triggers. That is not an even exchange. It is a conversion of four life into an entire opposing starting total, provided the battlefield remains favorable.

This is black design in an early, unusually clean form. Black obtains a rate the other colors cannot receive, but the card does not hide the bill. The strategic skill lies in recognizing when the bill is smaller than the advantage.

V · The Clock

The four-attack clock

Five power creates simple combat arithmetic. An unblocked Juzám ends a game from twenty life in four attacks. Any prior damage from Black Vise, Hypnotic Specter, Mishra's Factory, or Underworld Dreams shortens that clock.

Attack one

15 life

The opponent has already entered a range where two additional attackers or a burn spell can matter.

Attack two

10 life

The Djinn now represents a two-turn clock by itself and makes defensive exchanges increasingly desperate.

Attack three

5 life

Every blocker, removal spell, Maze activation, and racing calculation becomes mandatory.

The card’s strength was therefore not simply “good stats.” It reduced the opponent’s decision window. A deck using discard or land destruction could spend its early turns taking away answers and then present a threat that allowed almost no time to recover.

VI · The Commitment

Two black symbols shaped the shell

The mana cost {2}{B}{B} is permissive compared with four black mana, but it still rewards a deck built around Swamps, Mox Jet, Urborg, and black-producing dual lands. Juzám was not a generic artifact threat that every deck could adopt without cost.

That commitment aligned naturally with the cards black already wanted to play: Dark Ritual, Sinkhole, Hypnotic Specter, Mind Twist, Demonic Tutor, and efficient removal. The Djinn gave those disruptive openings a clock.

In multicolor lists, the double-black requirement encouraged decks to make black a primary color even when they splashed white for Swords to Plowshares and Disenchant, red for Lightning Bolt, or green for land destruction and recursion.

VII · The Acceleration

Dark Ritual alone is not enough

Juzám Djinn is often remembered as a turn-one Dark Ritual play, but one Swamp and one Ritual produce only three available mana after the Ritual is cast. The Djinn costs four. The famous opening requires one additional source.

Swamp + Ritual

Produces three available mana. Enough for Hypnotic Specter, but one short of Juzám.

Swamp + Mox Jet + Ritual

Produces four available mana and casts Juzám on the first turn.

Swamp + two Rituals

The first Ritual supplies mana for the second; five mana remains available, enough for Juzám with one mana left.

Land + Black Lotus

Four total mana casts Juzám immediately, with the land satisfying one of the black symbols if necessary.

The distinction matters because the card’s reputation sometimes compresses an entire powered opening into the phrase “Ritual out a Juzám.” The acceleration was real, but the most explosive starts depended on scarce artifacts or multiple cards.

VIII · The Test

The answers that mattered

Juzám Djinn’s five toughness invalidated the cleanest red answer of the era. One Lightning Bolt dealt only three damage. Psionic Blast still fell one short. Red decks frequently needed combat damage, two spells, or a specialized effect.

Black’s own Terror could not target a black creature. That made Juzám especially oppressive in black mirrors, where opposing decks could be forced toward Oubliette, Paralyze, Nevinyrral's Disk, Maze of Ith, or combat.

Swords to Plowshares remained the cleanest answer. It ignored toughness, cost one mana, and converted the Djinn into five life rather than allowing it to dominate combat. This interaction helped make white a natural partner for black Juzám decks: the splash answered opposing large creatures while protecting the deck from artifacts and enchantments black could not efficiently remove.

IX · The Ground

A giant without evasion

Juzám is not a perfect threat. It has no flying, trample, menace, or protection. A disposable creature can block it for a turn, Maze of Ith can neutralize its combat damage indefinitely, and Moat can remove it from combat entirely.

Old School battlefields also contain creatures designed to make ground combat awkward. Sedge Troll regenerates. Will-o'-the-Wisp flies and regenerates. Royal Assassin punishes attacking once the Djinn becomes tapped. A large body solves many problems, but not positional ones.

That is why the best Juzám decks rarely rely on the creature alone. Discard clears removal. Sinkhole attacks the mana needed to deploy blockers. Underworld Dreams or Black Vise creates a second axis of pressure. White removal opens the ground. The card is strongest when the rest of the deck turns “no evasion” into a temporary inconvenience.

X · The Cost

When the upkeep damage becomes real

The one-point trigger is small, not meaningless. It becomes dangerous when the game stalls, when multiple copies remain on the battlefield, or when the opponent is also attacking life totals with burn, Black Vise, Underworld Dreams, or evasive creatures.

A second Juzám doubles the clock but also doubles the upkeep damage. Against a deck capable of preventing combat, the controller may be forced to remove, sacrifice, or stop deploying additional copies. A player at three life cannot safely treat the trigger as flavor text.

Good Juzám play therefore requires a shift in perspective. Early in the game, life is fuel. Late in the game, the same trigger can become a deadline. The skill lies in knowing when the exchange rate has changed.

XI · The Home

The natural mono-black shell

Mono-black gives Juzám the mana consistency it wants and surrounds it with cards that attack the opponent before combat begins. Hypnotic Specter threatens random discard. Sinkhole and Strip Mine constrict mana. Mind Twist can remove an entire hand. Underworld Dreams pressures draw-heavy control decks.

The creature suite frequently combines the Djinn with Black Knight, Order of the Ebon Hand, Sengir Vampire, or artifact creatures. Small threats begin the race; Juzám makes the race difficult to reverse.

The deck’s weakness is structural. Black has limited answers to artifacts and enchantments, and some of its own premium removal fails against black creatures. Sideboards therefore lean heavily on Disk, Gloom, Terror, Oubliette, and color-specific hate, while splashes solve the permanent types mono-black cannot.

XII · The Lock

Nether Void turned size into inevitability

Nether Void taxes every spell by three generic mana. A deck that accelerates Juzám onto the battlefield first can then resolve Nether Void and transform the game into a nearly impossible race: the opponent must pay four mana for Swords to Plowshares while lands are being destroyed, but the 5/5 already exists and needs no further investment.

This is the ideal relationship between a lock piece and a threat. Nether Void does not win by itself. Juzám converts the delay into a four-turn ending. The Djinn’s low printed cost matters twice—first because it can enter before the lock, and second because fast mana makes that sequence plausible.

Early black-green-red land-destruction shells used Sinkhole, Ice Storm, Stone Rain, Strip Mine, and Black Vise to keep the opponent below the mana threshold needed to function beneath the enchantment. Juzám was the creature that made “eventually” arrive quickly.

XIII · The Pressure

Underworld Dreams and divided clocks

Underworld Dreams and Juzám Djinn seem to pull in different directions. One attacks through draw steps and punishes extra cards; the other attacks through combat. Together, they create a divided defense.

An opponent who uses removal on the Djinn may remain trapped beneath Dreams. An opponent who spends Disenchant on the enchantment may leave a 5/5 alive. Howling Mine effects, Timetwister, Wheel of Fortune, or ordinary draw steps continue shrinking the margin for racing.

The cost is that both cards ask for heavy black mana and neither stabilizes a losing battlefield. The shell succeeds when disruption creates the time for one or both clocks to matter.

XIV · The Splash

The white splash solved black’s blind spots

Black-white Juzám decks gain the two answers early black most wanted. Swords to Plowshares removes any opposing creature regardless of color or toughness. Disenchant handles artifacts and enchantments that mono-black might otherwise be unable to touch.

Scrubland, Mox Pearl, and City of Brass make the splash possible without abandoning double black. Some lists add Serra Angel as an evasive threat or Circle of Protection effects in the sideboard.

The result is less pure but more resilient. Juzám remains the efficient center of gravity, while white reduces the number of permanents that can invalidate it.

XV · The Record

The difference between fame and results

Juzám Djinn is remembered as though it dominated every early championship. The surviving record is more complicated. Many of the most famous 1994 and 1995 tournament decks were built around control engines, Armageddon, Necropotence, artifact mana, or efficient white and blue creatures rather than four Juzáms.

The card’s legend came from several sources at once: real strength in Type I and local competition, scarcity from a small first expansion, spectacular artwork, the visceral experience of facing a 5/5 far ahead of schedule, and the collector culture that formed around early rares.

That distinction makes Juzám historically interesting. Its reputation is not fraudulent; the creature genuinely established a new rate. But its cultural dominance became broader than its documented championship résumé. It was the creature players wanted, feared, traded for, and used as a reference point—even when other cards won the largest events.

XVI · The Return

Old School gave the card a second competitive life

As Vintage accelerated and creature quality improved, Juzám ceased to be a serious choice in unrestricted eternal competition. Old School 93/94 reversed that process by restoring the card pool in which its rate, mana requirements, and weaknesses made sense.

The modern Old School community does not merely preserve Juzám as a museum piece. It registers the card in mono-black, black-white, Mardu, Nether Void, and multicolor midrange decks. The exact legality of reprints and the restricted list varies by regional rules, but the original Arabian Nights card remains one of the format’s defining visual and strategic objects.

Old School also restores the original context of removal. Lightning Bolt still fails to kill it. Terror still cannot target it. Swords remains excellent. Maze remains frustrating. The creature’s power is not simulated by nostalgia; it reappears because the surrounding ecosystem has been reconstructed.

XVII · The Winner

A 2015 tournament winner

At a 2015 Swedish-rules Old School event in Ohio, Andrew Raichlin won with a black-white deck containing the full four copies of Juzám Djinn. The list combined Sinkhole and Hypnotic Specter with Underworld Dreams, then used white removal to cover the permanent types black struggled to answer.

The deck shows Juzám in a practical modern Old School role. It is not a solitary shrine to a famous card. It is one threat among several overlapping pressure systems: discard, mana denial, Dreams, Mishra’s Factory, and large creatures.

The four copies matter because they maximize the chance of following early disruption with the fastest reliable body in the deck. The sideboard then adjusts for red damage, white removal, opposing creatures, and artifact-heavy strategies.

XVIII · The Persistence

Mono-black still winning in 2024

Ishaan Dan Gluskinos won a 64-player X-point Old School online event in February 2024 with four Juzám Djinns in mono-black. The list is strikingly direct: sixteen creatures, Sinkhole, Underworld Dreams, Drain Life, a small fast-mana package, and twenty-three lands.

The result demonstrates that Juzám’s continued use is not merely ceremonial. In a rules environment designed around the early card pool, the original exchange remains competitive. The Djinn supplies the largest uncomplicated body, while Hypnotic Specter and Order of the Ebon Hand pressure different answers.

The list also illustrates how Old School deck building has evolved. The exact restricted structure and X-point system alter which broken cards can coexist, but Juzám requires no special exemption. It succeeds because four mana for a 5/5 is still a meaningful rate when the format’s answers and alternatives come from the same era.

XIX · The Descendant

The 2026 Mardu descendant

A 2026 Mardu Old School list by José Manuel Kiwi paired three Juzáms with Hypnotic Specter, Order of the Ebon Hand, Black Knight, Sedge Troll, Lightning Bolt, Swords to Plowshares, and Hymn to Tourach. It won a 20-player X-points event.

This shell makes the card’s modern niche explicit. Juzám is no longer expected to carry the entire deck’s identity. It is the top of an aggressive-disruptive curve, surrounded by efficient removal and resilient creatures.

The red and white splashes improve tactical flexibility, while the black core preserves the mana needed to cast the Djinn. Thirty-three years after Arabian Nights, the card still functions when the format asks the same essential question it was designed to answer: what is the most pressure black can place on the battlefield for four mana?

XX · The Artifact

From game piece to prestige object

Juzám Djinn became valuable before many later players encountered it as a playable card. Arabian Nights had a small print run, the card was rare, and the original illustration became instantly recognizable. Demand came from tournament players, collectors, set builders, and people who simply wanted the card that represented early creature power.

That prestige changed how the card was experienced. A Juzám in a deck was not only a game piece but a visible commitment to black, to Old School, and often to the physical history of Magic. Four copies formed an especially conspicuous display of both strategic intention and collection depth.

The card’s price can obscure its design. Beneath the market history is an unusually clean object: four mana, five power, five toughness, and one recurring life payment. The simplicity helped make it legible across decades even after the rate stopped being exceptional.

XXI · The Freeze

The Reserved List froze the supply

Juzám Djinn appears on Wizards of the Coast’s Reserved List. It therefore has never received another tournament-legal paper printing. The original Arabian Nights card remains the only normal-sized paper version legal for sanctioned constructed play.

Wizards did produce an oversized promotional version, which is not legal as an ordinary game card, and a digital printing in the original Magic Online Masters Edition. These releases preserve access to the image or game object in limited contexts without creating a regular paper reprint.

The policy permanently joined the card’s identity to the first expansion. Unlike a modern staple whose latest printing can replace an older copy, every paper player who wants Juzám must seek the same 1993 object.

XXII · The Face

Mark Tedin’s unforgettable face

Mark Tedin’s composition is one reason the card escaped the ordinary fate of obsolete creatures. The Djinn is not shown at a safe distance or framed as a complete anatomical study. It crowds the image, emerging through smoke and darkness with its face tilted toward the viewer.

The grin is the center of the painting. It does not communicate mindless rage. It suggests intelligence, appetite, and confidence—the expression of a creature that understands the bargain more clearly than its summoner does.

The artwork also complements the mechanical design. The card damages its controller every upkeep, and the image feels personal enough to make that consequence believable. Juzám is not a beast pointed toward the opponent. It is a dangerous ally standing close enough to hurt whoever called it.

XXIII · The Bloodline

The descendants of the bargain

Later black creatures repeatedly revisited Juzám’s equation: provide a body above the normal rate and attach a drawback that asks the controller to manage life, cards, permanents, or the possibility of losing.

Phyrexian Negator

Three mana for a 5/5 with trample, but incoming damage can dismantle the controller’s battlefield. The rate became even more aggressive and the risk more explosive.

Grinning Demon

Four mana for a 6/6 that deals two damage each upkeep, with morph providing another route onto the battlefield.

Plague Sliver

A direct 5/5-for-four echo whose upkeep damage scales with the number of Slivers controlled.

Abyssal Persecutor

Four mana for a 6/6 with flying and trample; the bargain shifts from life loss to the requirement that its controller eventually remove it.

Rotting Regisaur

Three mana for a 7/6, paying with a discarded card each upkeep rather than life.

Ravenous Giant

A red 5/5 for four with the same one-damage upkeep trigger and a composition that deliberately recalls the original.

Modern Horizons, 2019

Ravenous Giant remembers the pose

Ravenous Giant shifts the cost and upkeep trigger into red while visually echoing the looming, close-framed posture of Juzám Djinn. The homage works because the original image had become a recognizable piece of Magic’s visual language.

The descendants reveal how foundational the idea became. The specific rate changed, but the template remained: power now, consequences later.

XXIV · The Reversal

Power creep changed the verdict

In contemporary Magic, a four-mana 5/5 with no evasion, protection, card advantage, or immediate battlefield effect is not considered pushed. Creatures now compete with efficient removal and with threats that generate value when cast, when they enter, when they attack, or when they die.

This does not mean Juzám was secretly weak. Card strength is relational. In 1993, its body invalidated common damage spells and outclassed most creatures available at the same cost. In current Legacy or Commander, the same text competes with decades of accumulated design.

The card is therefore an unusually clear instrument for measuring creature inflation. Nothing about Juzám changed. Everything around it did.

XXV · The Habitats

Where Juzám still belongs

Defining

Old School 93/94

The card’s authentic competitive home, where the rate, removal, mana, and cultural meaning all remain relevant.

Legal, obsolete

Vintage and Legacy

Technically available but far below the efficiency and resilience required by contemporary eternal competition.

Expressive

Commander and Cube

A deliberate choice for theme, nostalgia, rarity, or a curated environment rather than raw efficiency.

Juzám was never reintroduced into a rotating paper environment, and the Reserved List prevents that route now. Its competitive afterlife is therefore concentrated in formats that intentionally preserve or curate early Magic.

XXVI · The Choice

Commander, Cube, and deliberate nostalgia

Commander offers countless black creatures that do more for the same mana. Juzám does not draw cards, recur itself, sacrifice another permanent, drain the table, or create a combo. Including it is an aesthetic and historical choice.

That choice can still be meaningful. A Djinn-themed deck, a Rabiah collection, a mono-black history deck, or an all-original-frame list may value what the card represents more than what it optimizes. Its simple upkeep trigger also interacts with lifelink, damage prevention, and effects that care about being dealt damage, though none transforms it into a modern staple.

Cube is a more natural laboratory. A designer can place Juzám beside period-appropriate removal and creatures, reconstruct an Old School power band, or intentionally test how far a once-elite rate has fallen. In a 1993–1996 cube, the card can again feel enormous.

XXVII · The Practice

How to pilot the card well

Clear the path

Use discard and land destruction before deploying Juzám when possible. The creature is most punishing when the opponent has neither the answer nor the mana to cast it.

Count both clocks

Track the opponent’s life and your own upkeep triggers. The card is a bargain only while your life total remains less important than the pressure it creates.

Respect Maze

Do not assume one large attacker is sufficient against Maze of Ith, Icy Manipulator, regenerators, or disposable blockers. Preserve alternate threats.

Sequence Rituals

One Dark Ritual does not reach four mana from a single Swamp. Confirm the actual mana before keeping a hand built around a mythical turn-one line.

Sideboard honestly

Against burn or stalled control games, trimming copies can be correct. Historical importance does not make the upkeep damage optional.

XXVIII · The Memory

What players remember

Juzám Djinn was the creature that made a binder page feel dangerous. Even before it was cast, the card announced that someone possessed a piece of Magic’s first mythology.
Compulsive Research · on the prestige of the original Arabian Nights rare

Players remember the card’s apparent impossibility: a 5/5 for four when Serra Angel cost five and Sengir Vampire cost five. They remember the dark mana symbols that could be difficult to read in a photograph, the grin, the accent in the name, and the sensation that Arabian Nights contained objects from a more dangerous version of the game.

Later generations often encountered Juzám through price guides, old magazines, advertisements, database searches, or stories from players who had wanted one in 1994. That secondhand fame mattered. The card became a legend even for people who never saw it in a tournament.

Its modern obsolescence sharpens rather than erases the memory. Juzám shows that power is historical. A card can stop being efficient and remain essential because it changed what efficiency once meant.

Printings and sources

Juzám Djinn has only one ordinary tournament-legal paper printing: Arabian Nights. An oversized promotional card reproduced the original object at display scale, and the first Magic Online Masters Edition later brought it to the digital game using the same Mark Tedin artwork. Those are the card’s three catalogued releases. The absence of a normal paper reprint is a consequence of the Reserved List.
