The Draw Seven That Was Never Fair

Timetwister gave both players seven new cards, but only one player chose when the old hands, used spells, and carefully depleted libraries would be folded into a new game.

I · The new hand

The draw seven that was never fair

Timetwister does not ask whether the game should start over. It asks whether you are ready to start over before your opponent is.

For three mana, each player takes every card in hand and every card in the graveyard, shuffles those resources back into the library, and draws seven new cards. On its face, the transaction appears almost ceremonially balanced. Both players surrender a hand. Both receive seven. Both recover the cards that had already been spent.

Magic has spent its entire history demonstrating why identical text applied to both players does not create an identical result.

The caster chooses the deck, the sequence, and the moment. One player may have emptied a hand of Moxen, rituals, tutors, and cheap interaction while the other still holds five carefully preserved answers. One player may have generated mana before casting Timetwister and be prepared to continue immediately; the other must wait for a turn that may never arrive. One player may want the graveyard returned because key restricted cards have already been used. The other may have spent several turns assembling a graveyard that functions as a second hand.

That tension made Timetwister the most disputed member of the Power Nine. It is less universally desirable than Ancestral Recall, Time Walk, Black Lotus, or the five Moxen. There are fair decks that would rather preserve their hand and graveyard. There are Cube drafts in which it can circle the table. Yet when a deck is built to exploit it, Timetwister performs an act no ordinary draw spell can match: it simultaneously becomes a refill, a recursion engine, a graveyard attack, and the beginning of another turn hidden inside the current one.

II · Limited Edition Alpha

Alpha’s reset button

Alpha contained several cards that treated the rules of Magic as raw material rather than settled law. Black Lotus accelerated mana before the first land. Time Walk inserted another turn. Ancestral Recall converted one card into three. Timetwister reached farther: it gathered the private zones where the game’s history was stored and rewrote them.

Richard Garfield’s earliest Magic was built for discovery. Players were expected to encounter strange cards in small local ecosystems and learn what they could do through play. In that environment, a three-mana global reset could appear whimsical. It prevented decking, recovered discarded spells, changed both hands, and created stories. Only later did competitive players formalize the central lesson: the player who initiates a symmetrical effect can build the entire deck around surviving—or exploiting—the symmetry.

Wizards eventually grouped Timetwister with the other eight cards known as the Power Nine, the most famous collection of early design excess. Unlike the six artifact-mana pieces, it does not fit every deck. Unlike Ancestral Recall, it is not straightforward card advantage. Its place comes from ceiling rather than consistency. Under the right conditions, it draws far more than three cards while undoing the costs paid to reach it.

Timetwister is the Power Nine card that most clearly asks a deck-building question before it gives a powerful answer.Symmetrical text, asymmetrical preparation
III · The resolution

What the card actually does

The modern Oracle text is compact, but the order matters. Each player shuffles their hand and graveyard into their library. Each then draws seven cards. Timetwister itself is not among the cards shuffled because a resolving spell remains on the stack until its instructions are complete. Only afterward is the sorcery placed into its owner’s graveyard.

The battlefield remains untouched. Lands, artifacts, creatures, enchantments, counters, and planeswalkers stay exactly where they were. Mana already floating in a player’s pool remains available until the step or phase ends. Exiled cards remain exiled. Commanders in the command zone remain there. Those exclusions explain much of the card’s brokenness. Timetwister does not literally restart the game; it preserves the caster’s developed board and mana while replacing the zones most likely to constrain further action.

It also differs from a normal wheel effect in the treatment of the graveyard. Wheel of Fortune discards hands and draws seven, leaving all spent cards in the graveyard. Timetwister returns those cards to the library. That can deny reanimation, threshold, flashback, escape, delve, and other graveyard plans. It can also restore restricted cards, tutors, counterspells, or win conditions that the caster expects to find again.

Before

The caster develops mana, empties cheap cards, and chooses a moment when the opponent’s hand or graveyard is more valuable.

Shuffle

Both hands and graveyards become libraries again; battlefield resources remain.

Draw seven

Both players receive the same quantity of cards, not the same opportunity to use them.

Continue

The caster may still have mana, storm count, artifacts, or a denial piece that converts the apparent reset into a one-sided turn.

IV · The illusion

Symmetry is not fairness

Magic’s symmetrical cards reward the player who controls context. Wrath of God destroys all creatures, but a control deck may have no creatures in play. Armageddon destroys all lands, but an aggressive deck may already have the only relevant threat. Timetwister gives everyone seven cards, but the caster decides when seven new cards are worth more than the cards already held.

The simplest imbalance is hand size. Casting Timetwister with one card remaining turns that final card into seven. An opponent with six cards also draws seven, gaining almost nothing in quantity while losing the quality of a hand that may have been carefully sculpted. Even when both players begin with small hands, board position matters. A player with five artifacts and two floating mana is not in the same situation as a player with three untapped lands who must pass after drawing.

Information matters too. The caster knows the deck contains Timetwister and can sequence around it. Tutors can place a desired card into hand before the old hand is shuffled away or find the Twister at the exact moment a reload is profitable. The opponent must make ordinary decisions under the possibility that all accumulated plans will suddenly be randomized.

V · The broken sequence

Empty the hand, then refill it

The classic Timetwister turn begins with acceleration. A normal player plays one land and is constrained by the number of cards that can be cast. A powered deck plays Moxen, Black Lotus, Mana Vault, Sol Ring, or rituals, converting the opening hand into battlefield resources and mana. Timetwister then replaces the cardboard that created those resources without removing the resources themselves.

This is why the spell belongs naturally beside the rest of the Power Nine. The Moxen and Lotus create the ability to unload a hand before an opponent can do the same. Timetwister rewards that acceleration with seven more cards. The new hand may contain further mana, interaction, a tutor, or the win condition. From the opponent’s perspective, the game has begun with the caster taking several opening hands in succession.

The sequence also exposes the difference between card disadvantage and resource conversion. Sacrificing Black Lotus or spending Dark Ritual normally trades cards for temporary mana. If Timetwister follows, the cards sacrificed to accelerate are shuffled back into the library while the mana has already performed its work. The reset does not refund everything—the original physical cards are no longer in hand—but it weakens the long-term cost of spending them aggressively.

The central combo principle: Timetwister is strongest when the caster has already converted the old hand into resources that the spell does not reset.
VI · The erased past

The graveyard disappears into the library

Timetwister’s relationship with the graveyard gives it a second strategic identity. It is not only a draw-seven spell. It is a mass reshuffle that denies both players access to cards that expected to remain in the graveyard.

Against a graveyard deck, this can be disruptive. Reanimation targets vanish. Flashback spells and escape fuel disappear. Delve becomes harder. A carefully stocked graveyard is transformed into an uncertain library, and the opponent draws only seven cards from the entire rebuilt deck.

For the caster, the reshuffle can create inevitability. Restricted cards that have already been cast—Ancestral Recall, Time Walk, Demonic Tutor, Black Lotus—return to the library. So do counterspells, removal, and win conditions. A control deck approaching exhaustion can acquire another cycle of answers. A combo deck can recover pieces that were countered, discarded, milled, or sacrificed.

The cost is that the caster also loses immediate graveyard access. Timetwister can conflict with Yawgmoth’s Will, delve spells, escape, flashback, and recursion engines. Good sequencing often means extracting value from the graveyard first, then casting Timetwister when the spent zone has become less useful than a fresh library.

VII · The first strange deck

The infinite-recursion dream

Richard Garfield’s account of Magic’s creation describes an early league deck built by Charlie Catin that he called the “Infinite Recursion Deck.” The deck sought to prevent attacks, use Swords to Plowshares, and eventually cast Timetwister so that spent cards could be shuffled back into a fresh library.

The anecdote matters because it captures Timetwister before the modern vocabulary of combo, prison, and inevitability had fully formed. The card immediately suggested that a deck did not have to accept the graveyard as permanent history. If the game could be stabilized, every answer might be drawn again.

Later rules wording and competitive structures made the loops more precise, but the underlying fantasy remained: build a game state that favors you, then repeatedly renew the hidden resources while leaving that favorable state intact. Timetwister does not need to create a literal infinite loop to feel recursive. Recovering Ancestral Recall and Time Walk once can be enough.

Mark Tedin’s original Timetwister artworkThe image does not depict a clean clock or calendar. It presents time as a turbulent space folding around itself—an appropriate vision for a spell that preserves the battlefield while throwing every private plan back into the library.
VIII · The first champion

The first World Champion

Zak Dolan’s 1994 World Championship deck contained the full restricted architecture of early Magic: Black Lotus, the five Moxen, Ancestral Recall, Time Walk, and Timetwister. The surrounding list was a U/W/G control deck of extraordinary singleton density, including Stasis, Kismet, Serra Angel, Wrath of God, Armageddon, Regrowth, Recall, Library of Alexandria, and numerous artifacts.

In that environment Timetwister performed several roles at once. It repaired a hand after the jewelry had been deployed. It recycled one-of answers and power. It complicated decking in long control games. It also interacted naturally with Regrowth and Recall, which could recover the best spell before or after the reset.

Dolan’s deck illustrates why the card cannot be evaluated as a generic draw spell. The surrounding cards determine whether both players receive seven ordinary cards or whether one player receives another chance to draw the most powerful restricted cards ever printed.

IX · Controlled renewal

The Deck and controlled renewal

Brian Weissman’s The Deck made card advantage, sequencing, and inevitability into an explicit control doctrine. Timetwister appears inside that doctrine as a dangerous tool rather than an automatic play.

The Deck often wanted to preserve a superior hand built through Jayemdae Tome, Braingeyser, and Library of Alexandria. Giving an opponent seven cards could undo the careful work of Mind Twist and Disrupting Scepter. Timetwister therefore demanded timing. It was strongest after the control player had converted cards into durable permanents, exhausted key resources, or reached a point where recycling the library mattered more than protecting the current hand.

That restraint is one of the card’s defining lessons. Timetwister is powerful enough to belong in the Power Nine and conditional enough to punish a careless caster. A player who casts it merely because three mana is available may replace a winning hand with uncertainty and rescue an opponent who was running out of options.

X · The family name

The wheel family

Magic players eventually adopted “wheel” as the general term for effects that replace hands, taking the name from Wheel of Fortune. Timetwister is part of that family but behaves differently from its most famous red relative.

Wheel of Fortune discards hands, fueling graveyards and triggering effects that care about discard. Timetwister shuffles those hands away, avoiding discard triggers and erasing graveyards. Wheel can be cast to force an opponent to discard a sculpted hand permanently. Timetwister denies the hand but returns its cards to the library, where they may be drawn again.

Those differences determine deck fit. Reanimator and madness strategies often prefer true discard. Recursion loops and graveyard denial may prefer Timetwister. Storm decks care about the low cost and seven new cards. Commander decks may value the ability to prevent decking or recover a finite library. The superficial sentence “everyone draws seven” conceals several distinct resource systems.

XI · The same turn

The draw seven inside the combo turn

Combo decks reveal the card’s most violent interpretation. The opponent’s seven cards are only theoretically equivalent if the opponent receives another turn. Timetwister can be cast in the middle of a sequence after mana has been generated, storm count has been built, and tutors have located the engine. The caster immediately sees seven new cards and continues.

Three mana is crucial. Later draw-sevens often cost five, six, or more, exile themselves, require setup, or impose conditions. Timetwister can be paid for with a single Black Lotus, three Moxen, a ritual plus a land, or Tolarian Academy. It is cheap enough that the refill does not consume the whole turn.

Because the graveyard is shuffled away, the spell requires care beside Yawgmoth’s Will. A Storm pilot may use Will first, replaying mana and tutors, then Twister after the graveyard has been harvested. Alternatively, Timetwister can recover cards for a later cycle. The decision is less about raw hand size than which zone currently contains the most valuable version of the deck.

The opponent also draws seven cards. The unfair deck’s answer is simple: those cards are irrelevant if the game ends before they can be cast.The combo interpretation of symmetry
XII · Seven becomes zero

When seven becomes zero

Later Magic printed permanents that expose the darkest possible reading of a wheel. Narset, Parter of Veils limits opponents to one draw each turn. Hullbreacher replaces extra opposing draws with Treasure for its controller. Notion Thief steals those draws entirely.

Beside these cards, Timetwister stops pretending to be symmetrical. The caster receives seven while the opponent receives one, zero, or a dramatically altered result. Hullbreacher can also generate a pile of Treasure, turning the opponent’s lost hand into mana for the caster’s new one.

These combinations explain why draw replacement and mass hand replacement are monitored so carefully. The original card was already undercosted when both players drew seven. Once a permanent changes the second half of the sentence, the spell resembles a three-mana Mind Twist plus Ancestral Recall multiplied several times over.

XIII · The exception

The Power Nine exception

Timetwister occupies a unique place in Commander. The official Commander banned list includes Black Lotus, Ancestral Recall, Time Walk, and all five original Moxen—but not Timetwister. It is therefore the only member of the Power Nine legal in the format under the current list.

That legality gave the card an afterlife far larger than its narrow Vintage role might suggest. In multiplayer, a single spell can replace four hands, reload a combo player, recycle a library, or disrupt several graveyards at once. Competitive Commander decks can exploit its low mana value, interaction with Lion’s Eye Diamond, and compatibility with draw-denial pieces.

The format also magnifies the social and economic questions around the card. Timetwister is Reserved List power with an enormous physical price, yet Commander communities often use proxies or tournament policies that make the game object more accessible. The card can be simultaneously legal, strategically desirable, and practically unavailable in original tournament-legal paper.

Its Commander legality is not proof that it is harmless. It reflects the format’s distinct ban philosophy and the fact that multiplayer symmetry gives opponents more collective resources—until a deck turns that symmetry off.

XIV · The restricted reload

The restricted reload

Timetwister remains restricted in Vintage, meaning only one copy may appear across the main deck and sideboard. It is banned in Legacy alongside the rest of the original power package.

Restriction fits the card’s identity. Four copies would make opening-hand reloads too consistent and recursion too easy. One copy becomes a tool chosen by decks that can exploit it: Tinker shells, Storm, Paradoxical Outcome, certain control decks, and strategies built around Hullbreacher or graveyard reset.

It is not universal even in Vintage. Some decks rely heavily on their graveyards. Some protect carefully sculpted hands. Some cannot afford to give an opponent seven chances to find Force of Will or another piece of free interaction. Timetwister’s absence from many successful lists does not diminish its ceiling; it clarifies that the ninth piece of Power asks for more context than the other eight.

Contemporary official deck examples still show the card in both control and combo-control shells. An Esper Lurrus deck can use it after exchanges of cheap interaction. Grixis Tinker can combine it with Hullbreacher, artifact mana, Tinker, and Yawgmoth’s Will. The same Alpha sentence continues to find new asymmetries.

Timetwister also survives in Old School variants according to local rules, in Powered Cube, and in unsanctioned proxy environments. Each context values a different part of the card. Old School emphasizes original engines and hand management. Cube asks whether the drafter can build enough fast mana and combo density to justify a symmetrical refill. Commander emphasizes multiplayer scaling and combo loops. Vintage uses the precise restricted tool.

XVI · The ninth piece

The strange Power Nine draft pick

Powered Cube reveals the difference between reputation and deck fit. Wizards’ Arena Powered Cube data published in 2026 showed Timetwister going last in a draft six times. The other pieces of power generally remained early picks.

That result is not an argument that the card is weak. It demonstrates that a symmetrical draw seven needs architecture. A fair creature deck may not want to give the opponent a new hand. A graveyard deck may hate the reshuffle. A control deck may prefer the cards already selected. Storm, artifact combo, and decks with draw-denial pieces can take it much earlier because they are capable of converting the text into something closer to one-sided card advantage.

Timetwister is therefore a useful test of powered-card evaluation. A drafter who sees the Power Nine label and ignores context may end up helping the opponent. A drafter who recognizes the correct shell receives one of the cheapest full-hand reloads ever printed.

XVII · Design after Alpha

The descendants that learned restraint

Later draw-sevens preserve pieces of Timetwister while adding costs or escape valves. Diminishing Returns costs four and exiles ten cards from the caster’s library. Time Spiral costs six, untaps lands, and exiles itself so it cannot be looped as easily. Echo of Eons costs six but offers flashback for three. Memory waits on the back half of a split card and costs six.

Other designs imitate the hand replacement without restoring the graveyard. Wheel of Fortune, Windfall, Reforge the Soul, and Wheel of Misfortune all create new hands through different pricing and political structures. Each descendant selects which dangerous property to keep: low cost, seven cards, graveyard recycling, mana refund, or asymmetrical potential.

Time Spiral’s self-exile is especially revealing. Wizards commentary has noted that removing itself prevents the repeated draw-seven recursion available to Timetwister. Even at six mana, free land untapping proved powerful enough to dominate environments. Alpha’s three-mana version had supplied the full lesson decades earlier.

The disciplined descendant

Time Spiral

Six mana, self-exile, and an untap clause transformed Timetwister’s reset into a combo engine with a built-in limit on recursive reuse.

XVIII · The object

The image of time folding inward

Mark Tedin’s Alpha artwork gives Timetwister a texture unlike the clean iconography of the Moxen or Black Lotus. The scene feels unstable and almost geological: forms curve around a central distortion while color and scale resist a single reading. It looks less like traveling through time than watching reality become something that can be shuffled.

The card’s name contributes to the effect. “Timetwister” is not a conventional fantasy spell such as Counterspell or Fireball. It names a process, an engine, and an impossible object at once. The word implies that chronology is being physically manipulated.

As a collectible, Timetwister shares the Reserved List status of the other original Power Nine. Wizards’ reprint policy prevents a tournament-legal, functionally identical physical reprint. Digital releases and non-tournament products have allowed more players to see the card, but the original paper object remains scarce.

That scarcity is particularly strange because Commander keeps the card legal. For most Power Nine cards, price and format prohibition point in the same direction. Timetwister invites players to register it in the game’s most popular casual format while the original object remains beyond ordinary acquisition.

Timetwister is remembered less as “draw seven” than as the moment the game’s past stops belonging to either player.

Printings and sources

Timetwister first appeared as a rare in Limited Edition Alpha and continued through Beta and Unlimited. It later appeared digitally in Vintage Masters and in non-tournament products that do not violate the Reserved List’s tournament-legal reprint restriction.
